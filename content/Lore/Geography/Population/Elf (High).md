Children of the solar celestials, created to tend their libraries and serve the true elves as heralds, high elves remember a better age. Their histories speak of a time before the coming of humans and war, when the celestials still walked the world and all that mattered was art and beauty.

In the millennia since their creators retired to Arcadia, the high elves have built a civilization of their own, dwelling primarily among the ruins of fallen celestial sky cities. With no creators left to please, they continue as they once did, collecting lore and knowledge, venerating art, and turning ever inward, growing more distrustful of outsiders with each passing generation.

On High Elves:
“They’re so beautiful,” Wenna said. “It’s hard to imagine we’re in danger.”

“It’s not that hard,” Dade said darkly.

The five children stood in the center of a wide, circular courtyard open to the sky, their wode elf escort, Llyander, at their side.

Lord Tear, King of the High Elves, sat upon a marble throne with the Codex Dryadalis resting in his lap. He had not spoken since Llyander delivered their speech and presented the scroll. Around them, nobles, courtiers, and learned sages gathered to watch. Implacable warriors in golden plate, etched with fine filigree, stood guard along the perimeter.

“They seem like…” Meliora said, searching for the words.

“Like the lords of all the world,” Wenna said, her voice filled with awe.

“And we are their prisoners,” Jeremy said, glancing at his brother Dade and at Credan beside him.

“You’re not prisoners,” Llyander said quietly. “You are guests. You’re safer here than you would be even in your own homes.”

“Yes,” Jeremy said, eyeing the nearest guards with their long spears and swords. “We feel very safe.”

“Who are you kidding?” Dade said. “Everyone knows how much elves hate humans.”

At this, Lord Tear exchanged a look with Llyander, consort to Queen Imyrr. It was a knowing look, tinged with sadness. Then the king broke his silence.

“Show me an elf who hates humans,” he said, his voice deep and resonant, “and I will show you an elf who loved a human and watched them grow old and die.”

He looked at the children for the first time and offered a melancholy smile.

“Love is like sunlight for us. We love completely, but rarely. And the loss of it means an eternity of grief.”

The king tapped the scroll lightly against his lap, as though arriving at a decision.

“Well done, consort. Young humans, your escort seeks to shame me. They know they could have taken this prize to their queen and earned her favor. Instead, Llyander brought it to me in the hope that, by doing so, they might grant me the power to cast off the yoke of Amergin’s rule. Long has Llyander resented the decision I made and sought to change it…”

He glanced at Llyander.

“…by changing my mind.”

Llyander inclined their head, silently acknowledging the truth.

“Alas, your escort’s efforts are for naught.” The king’s expression softened. He lifted the heavy scroll. “But this is not nothing. We made a treaty with Amergin to deliver unto him any artifacts our searchers recover from the ruins of this city. He benefits more from this bargain than we. But this…” He held the codex a little higher. “This, methinks, will remain with us.”

“It was written by my grandmother’s grandmother, in the youth of the world. And there are some things that must be denied the Redwood Grove, even should they violate the treaty.”

Llyander turned to the children, smiling brightly, their eyebrows lifting in playful triumph. Wenna and Credan could not help but smile in return.

“Well, you see, children?” Llyander said. “We only have more to do, not everything to do.”

The king rose.

A herald beside the throne stepped forward and proclaimed, “Gather and attend! The Lord of Fallen Skanley, Morning Dew on a Single Leaf Like a Tear from the Sun, speaks. And know his word is law!”
